editor guide

composition
crop drag to draw a region. choose a preset aspect ratio or draw freely. apply or cancel with the buttons below the canvas.
transform rotate 90° cw/ccw, flip h/v. straighten corrects a tilted horizon. perspective correction drags four corner handles to fix converging lines — commit bakes it and auto-crops black corners.
grid rule of thirds, golden ratio, diagonals, or 4×4. toggle with the checkbox in the top bar.
tone & colour
presets 12 built-in film looks. save your current adjustments as a named preset and recall them later. presets are stored in your browser.
adjust exposure · contrast · highlights · shadows · whites · blacks · fade · temperature · tint · vibrance · saturation · clarity · texture · dehaze · sharpen · masking · luma NR · color NR · grain. click any value to type it directly. double-click a slider to reset it.
sharpening masking limits sharpening to edges only. at 0 every pixel is sharpened equally; at 100 only strong edges are sharpened — smooth areas like sky and skin are left untouched.
luminosity mask limits all adjustments to a tonal range. set a floor and ceiling to target shadows, midtones, or highlights. feather softens the transition at each boundary.
tone curve click the curve to add points; drag to reshape. double-click a point to remove it. switch channels (composite, R, G, B) with the tabs. the histogram of the current channel is shown behind the curve.
hsl hue, saturation, and luminance across 8 colour bands: red, orange, yellow, green, aqua, blue, purple, magenta.
color grading drag the colour wheel to tint shadows, midtones, and highlights independently. the balance slider shifts the shadow/highlight boundary.
local adjustments
dodge & burn paint directly on the image to lighten (dodge), darken (burn), or restore (erase). erase blends pixels back toward the state before any dodge/burn was applied. adjust size with [ and ], tonal range with the shadows / midtones / highlights buttons.
masks non-destructive local adjustments. gradient masks apply from a full-effect line to a zero-effect line. radial masks apply inside an ellipse. each mask has its own exposure, highlights, shadows, saturation, and temperature. drag handles on the canvas to reposition. invert to flip the affected area. feather softens the edge.
finishing
vignette darkens the edges of the frame. feather controls how soft the gradient is; roundness blends between an ellipse (follows all edges) and a circle.
border solid-colour border around the image. link h & v to resize both axes together. colour picker sets the border colour.
label stamp text in permanent marker. appears in the bottom border area — increase vertical border for space. adjust size, horizontal position, and colour.
tools & view
compare drag the split divider to compare before and after side by side. press W to toggle.
before / after hold Y to temporarily view the original. release to return to edited.
clipping red zebra = blown highlights. blue zebra = crushed shadows. toggle with \ or the checkbox in the top bar.
color sampler hover over the image to read the RGB and HSL values under your cursor. useful for checking skin tone ratios and neutral greys.
histogram live RGB histogram in the sidebar. auto-enhance button sets blacks, whites, and exposure based on the 2nd and 98th percentile of the image.
export
format PNG for lossless; JPEG with quality 60–100 for sharing. scale sets the output resolution (25–100% of full size).
output sharpening applied after downscaling, on export only. compensates for softness introduced by resampling. separate from the adjustment sharpening slider.

a suggested order for editing a photograph. you don't have to follow every step — skip what doesn't apply. the order matters because earlier decisions affect how later adjustments look.

01 · composition first
crop and straighten before anything else
Decide your frame before touching tone or colour. Everything downstream — vignette placement, mask positions, your sense of what's bright or dark — depends on the final composition. Straighten the horizon in transform if the camera was tilted. Correct converging verticals with perspective if you're shooting architecture.
02 · foundation
set white balance, then exposure
White balance first — get the neutrals right (grey should look grey, whites should look white). Use the temperature and tint sliders, or the eyedropper on a neutral area. Then set exposure so the overall brightness feels right. Use the histogram as a guide: avoid clipping the highlights or crushing the shadows unless it's intentional.
03 · tone shaping
contrast, highlights, shadows, whites, blacks
Contrast adds punch but can crush shadows — pair it with highlights and shadows to recover detail at the extremes. Whites and blacks set the true endpoints of the tonal range. Fade lifts the blacks to create a matte, film-like look. The tone curve gives you precise control over specific tonal regions — a gentle S-curve adds contrast and character without affecting the endpoints.
04 · colour
global colour, then specific adjustments
Start broad: vibrance adds saturation to muted colours without oversaturating already-strong ones. Saturation affects everything equally. Then go narrow with HSL — shift, boost, or pull back specific colour bands (sky blues, foliage greens, skin tones). Color grading lets you push shadows cool and highlights warm, or any combination. Use the color sampler to check that skin tones have a healthy ratio (red slightly higher than green, green higher than blue).
05 · detail
sharpening and noise reduction
Apply noise reduction before sharpening — NR smooths out sensor noise; sharpening then enhances real edges rather than amplifying noise. Use the masking slider with sharpen to limit it to edges only, protecting smooth areas like skin and sky. Clarity adds localised contrast at a mid-frequency scale. Texture is subtler and great for preserving fine detail like fabric or bark.
06 · local adjustments
dodge, burn, and masks
These are your most powerful and time-consuming tools — apply them after global edits are settled. Dodge and burn guide the viewer's eye: lighten where you want attention, darken what's competing. Use the tonal range selector to limit strokes to highlights, midtones, or shadows. Gradient and radial masks let you apply targeted exposure or colour corrections — darkening a blown sky, warming a foreground, isolating the subject from the background.
07 · finishing
vignette, then border and label if needed
Vignette is the last adjustment before export. A subtle darkening of the corners draws the eye inward without the viewer noticing. Feather keeps it soft; roundness controls the shape. Add a border if you're presenting the image with breathing room. Add a label if you want to watermark or caption it.
08 · export
choose format, scale, and output sharpening
JPEG at quality 85–92 is right for most web use. PNG if you need pixel-perfect lossless output. If you're exporting at less than 100% scale, apply a small amount of output sharpening to compensate for softness introduced by resampling — it's applied after the resize, so it's separate from the adjustment sharpening. Download, and you're done.
